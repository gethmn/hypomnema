# Braising

A two-stage moist-heat technique: sear the food in fat to develop
crust and flavor, then cook submerged in liquid at low temperature
until tender. Best for collagen-rich cuts that benefit from long,
gentle conversion of connective tissue into gelatin.

## Cuts that braise well

Beef short rib, lamb shank, pork shoulder, oxtail, and chicken thighs
all reward braising. Lean cuts — chicken breast, pork loin — turn dry
and stringy under the same treatment; reserve them for quicker
techniques.

## Method

Pat the meat very dry, season generously, and sear in a heavy pot
until the surface is deeply browned on every face. Remove to a plate,
sweat aromatics in the rendered fat, deglaze with wine or stock to
lift the fond, return the meat, and add enough liquid to come about
halfway up the meat's height — never fully submerged.

Cover and cook at 150 °C for two to three hours, depending on cut and
size. The dish is done when a fork slides into the meat with no
resistance. Rest in the cooking liquid for a few minutes before
serving so the surface re-absorbs moisture from the braise.
