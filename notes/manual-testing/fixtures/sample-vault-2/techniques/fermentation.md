# Fermentation

Controlled microbial activity that transforms food through the
metabolic byproducts of bacteria, yeasts, or molds. Examples in a
home kitchen include yogurt, sauerkraut, kimchi, miso, sourdough
starter, and lacto-pickles.

## The lacto-fermentation path

Salt-tolerant *Lactobacillus* species outcompete spoilage organisms in
a salty anaerobic environment. The classic vegetable ferment uses 2
percent salt by weight relative to the vegetable plus its brine — the
salt halts spoilage long enough for native lactic-acid bacteria to
colonize and acidify the brine.

## Temperature

Most vegetable ferments work in the 18 to 22 °C range; warmer
ferments move faster but more reliably overshoot. Sourdough starters
prefer the same range. Miso and other longer ferments tolerate
higher temperatures during their initial active phase, then cool for
storage.

## Signs of a healthy ferment

Active bubbling, a clean tart smell, and clear or lightly cloudy
brine. Furry mold on the surface or a putrid smell signals that
spoilage organisms outpaced the lactobacilli — discard and start
over with cleaner equipment and fresher salt.
